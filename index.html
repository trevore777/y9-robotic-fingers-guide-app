    Install Autodesk Fusion on a Mac

    Follow this tutorial before beginning the Robotic Fingers CAD lessons. Use your school-approved Autodesk Education account process. Do not choose a paid commercial subscription unless your teacher specifically instructs you to.

    Important: Autodesk screens and eligibility steps can change. If your screen looks different, stop and ask your teacher rather than entering payment details.

    Make sure you have internet access and permission to install software. If your Mac is managed by your school, Autodesk Fusion may already be available through the school's software/self-service system.

    2

    Open Autodesk Education

    Use Autodesk's official Education page for Fusion. Sign in or create an Autodesk account using the process approved by your school.

    Open Autodesk Education — Fusion ↗
    3

    Sign in and confirm education access

    Sign in with your Autodesk account. Follow Autodesk's education eligibility prompts if they appear. Use accurate school and student information. Ask your teacher if you are unsure which email or school details to use.

    Do not enter payment details for this class installation unless your teacher/school has specifically instructed you to do so.
    4

    Download Fusion for macOS

    From the Autodesk Education/Fusion download area, choose the macOS version when prompted. The installer will normally appear in your Downloads folder.

    5

    Run the installer

    Open Finder → Downloads, open the Autodesk Fusion installer, and follow the on-screen installation instructions. macOS may ask for permission or an administrator password on managed devices.

    If installation is blocked, do not try to bypass school security settings. Ask your teacher or IT support.

    6

    Open Fusion and sign in

    Open Autodesk Fusion from Applications or Spotlight Search. Sign in using the same Autodesk account used for education access. Allow the application to complete any first-launch setup or updates.

    7

    Complete the 5-minute installation test

    2. Start a sketch on a flat plane.
    3. Draw a rectangle.
    4. Finish the sketch.
    5. Extrude the rectangle to make a simple 3D block.
    6. Save the design as Fusion Installation Test – Your Name.
    8

    Show your teacher

    Show your saved 3D test model to your teacher. Once checked, you are ready to begin the Robotic Fingers Fusion 360 modelling lessons.

    Week 1 — Entry Event + Prosthetics Research

    Task 1 Suggested Answers

    1. Which hand was damaged and how many fingers were damaged?
      Use the answer from the entry event video shown in class. Students should identify the specific hand and number of damaged fingers accurately from the source.
    2. When did the accident happen and what country was he in?
      Students should record the date/time period and country given in the video. Accept accurate paraphrasing if the answer is clearly sourced.
    3. What functionality did the prosthetic finger allow him to do again?
      It restored fine motor control and the ability to perform tasks requiring finger movement, especially piano playing or similar precise hand movement.
    4. What aspects of the prosthetic allowed him to forget about his disability?
      Comfort, natural movement, reliable control, realistic function, and the ability to complete meaningful everyday or musical tasks.
    5. What type of sensor allowed finger movement?
      Likely EMG/electromyography muscle sensors if the video refers to detecting muscle signals. Accept “muscle sensor” if used by the video.
    6. You need full __________ to play the piano.
      Likely “control”, “movement”, or “finger movement”, depending on the wording of the video.
    7. Why was machine learning needed?
      To interpret complex muscle/sensor signals and translate them into accurate, controlled finger movement.
    8. What song did the Grade 9 team create?
      Use the class project example shown to students. Accept the exact song used in your class demonstration.
    9. How many notes were needed?
      For the main project, students use three notes: C, D and E. Advanced teams may combine projects for six notes.

    Teacher Checkpoints

    • Task 1 completed with answers in full sentences.
    • Task 2 includes six robotic/prosthetic hand examples.
    • Each image has four meaningful annotations.
    • Annotations refer to design features, not just labels.

    Common Issues

    • Students paste images without sources.
    • Annotations describe appearance only, not function.
    • Students do not explain how research informs their own design.

    Week 2 — Fusion 360: Finger, Servo Mount and Assembly

    Expected Evidence

    • Screenshot of robotic finger model.
    • Screenshot of servo mount model.
    • Screenshot of assembled finger, servo and mount.
    • Student name visible in the top-right of Fusion 360 screenshots.

    Correct Understanding

    The robotic finger must be shaped to allow movement from the servo. The servo mount must hold the servo securely. The assembly should show how mechanical movement will transfer from the servo to the finger.

    Teacher Questions

    • How does your finger design allow movement?
    • How does the servo mount keep the motor stable?
    • What would happen if the servo was not fixed securely?

    Benchmark Standard

    By the end of Week 2, students should have Part 1 complete and Tasks 3–5 substantially complete or ready for final screenshots.

    Week 3 — Switch Mount Design

    Expected Evidence

    • Team members recorded.
    • Switch mount sketch or screenshot.
    • Measurements included.
    • Design allows switch position adjustment.
    • Full digital assembly includes Arduino, servo-finger units and switch mounts.

    Correct Understanding

    The switch mount must allow accurate alignment between finger movement and switch contact. A strong response explains measurement, adjustment, testing and refinement.

    Common Issues

    • Switch is fixed in one position with no adjustment.
    • Students model a mount but do not explain how it works.
    • Base layout does not leave enough space for Arduino, wires or fingers.

    Week 4 — Digital Manufacturing Preparation

    Expected Evidence

    • Mount base with holes screenshot.
    • DXF export evidence for laser cutting.
    • Research explaining FDM 3D printing.
    • Research explaining CO₂ laser cutting.

    Correct Answers

    FDM 3D printing: A thermoplastic filament is heated and extruded layer by layer to form a 3D object.

    CO₂ laser cutting: A focused laser beam cuts or engraves sheet material by burning, melting or vaporising the material along a programmed path.

    DXF file: A 2D vector file used by CAD/CAM systems and laser cutters to define cut lines and shapes.

    Week 5 — Manufacture + Servo Control

    Expected Evidence

    • Laser cutting file submitted.
    • 3D print file submitted.
    • Manufactured parts photographed.
    • Arduino connected and servo tested.
    • Servo horn alignment documented.

    Correct Understanding

    A servo motor rotates to a controlled angle. Testing at 0°, 90° and 180° helps students understand range of movement. Zeroing places the servo in a known starting position before attaching the horn or finger mechanism.

    Common Troubleshooting

    • Servo does not move: check power, ground, signal pin and code upload.
    • Servo moves backwards: reverse angle values in code or reposition the horn.
    • Finger misses switch: adjust mount position or servo horn alignment.

    Week 6 — Servo Control + Build Refinement

    Expected Evidence

    • Photo of zeroed finger position.
    • Explanation of Arduino as the control system.
    • Explanation of mechatronics: mechanical, electrical and software systems working together.
    • Refinement notes showing testing and adjustment.

    Teacher Questions

    • What changed after testing?
    • Why was that change needed?
    • How does your evidence show improvement?

    Week 7 — Makey Makey + GarageBand Setup

    Expected Evidence

    • Photo of Makey Makey wired to project.
    • GarageBand notes created: C, D and E for the three-finger version.
    • QwertyGo explanation.
    • Robot ready for music photo.

    Correct Understanding

    The Makey Makey acts like a keyboard input device. When the robotic finger activates a switch, the circuit triggers a keyboard input. QwertyGo maps keyboard inputs to sound samples or notes, and GarageBand produces the musical sound.

    Week 8 — Creating Music + Final Demonstration

    Expected Evidence

    • Song name and note sequence.
    • Final MP4 video or link.
    • Minimum 20 finger strokes for the main performance.
    • Arduino code pasted into the student evidence response.
    • Evaluation against criteria for success.

    High-Quality Student Response

    A strong response explains the relationship between design, manufacturing, electronics and programming. It uses specific evidence, such as screenshots, photos, code and testing notes, to justify how well the final robotic fingers met the success criteria.
